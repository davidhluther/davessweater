# Is Ray's Weather accurate? We scored it for 118 days

Mostly, but it trails the free apps. Over 118 scored days, Ray's Weather averages 71.0 of 100 against verified actuals, while Open-Meteo, which is free, averages 92.0. That is a gap of about 21 points a day.

## Is Ray's Weather accurate?

Ray's Weather is accurate more often than not (71.0 of 100 on our scale is a passing grade), but it is the least accurate of the three forecasts we track every day for Boone. Across 118 scored days, Open-Meteo averages 92.0 and Apple Weather 88.3, both of them free. Ray's, the paid local incumbent, sits at 71.0.

For two decades Ray's Weather has been the forecast of record for the High Country, and there is a reason people trust it. The local reputation is earned, and its hyperlocal read on mountain weather is genuinely good. We wondered whether "trusted" and "most accurate" were the same word, so we started scoring every forecast against what the sky actually did. This post is a data-backed review of Ray's Weather Boone forecast, not a swipe at it.

### What do 118 days of scoring show?

Ray's has been scored on 118 days. It landed in the Right band (75+) on 48 of them, Meh (60–74) on 47, and Wrong (under 60) on 23. Open-Meteo, over the identical 118 days, was Right on nearly every one and never once graded Wrong. The pattern is steady, not a fluke of one bad week.

### How do you read the 71.0 average?

A 71.0 means Ray's forecasts land, on average, within striking distance of the actual highs, lows, wind, and precipitation, but drift wider than the free apps do. Most of the daily gap comes from temperature misses and, on wet days, missing precipitation totals he never publishes. One decimal, tracked daily, no rounding games.

## How do we score Ray's Weather's accuracy?

We capture each forecast around midday, then score it against the next day's verified actuals on a fixed 100-point scale. Temperature carries the most weight, wind and precipitation the rest. The same model runs on every forecaster, so the comparison is apples to apples. Full detail lives on [how the 100-point scoring works](/methodology).

### How does the 100-point model work?

Points break down like this: high temp 30, low temp 30, wind 20, precip type 10, precip amount 10. Temperature dominates because it is what people plan around, and it is where forecasts most visibly succeed or fail.

| Category | Max points | Full credit within |
|---|---|---|
| High temp | 30 | 2°F |
| Low temp | 30 | 2°F |
| Wind speed | 20 | 3 mph |
| Precip type | 10 | exact match |
| Precip amount | 10 | snow-aware (rain ±0.1", snow ±max(1", 20%)) |

### Where do the actuals come from?

Actuals come from the Open-Meteo historical archive, disclosed openly on our methodology page. That creates one honest wrinkle: Open-Meteo grading against an Open-Meteo archive is a form of self-grading, so treat its number as a strong short-range benchmark rather than a perfectly independent one. Ray's and Apple Weather have no such advantage: they are scored against the same archive from the outside.

### How does the implied-zero rule for precip amounts work?

Ray's Weather never publishes a numeric precip amount. Under our rule, a "no precip" forecast counts as a zero-inch call, so Ray's earns the amount points on dry days he calls dry. On days he forecasts rain or snow without a number, he forfeits that field. A source cannot bank points by leaving the hard number blank. It cuts both ways, and it is the fair reading.

### Why do Open-Meteo and Apple Weather pull ahead?

Most of the margin is temperature. The free models nail highs and lows inside the 2°F tolerance far more often, and they publish numeric precipitation totals that earn or lose the amount field honestly. Ray's leans on descriptive precip language, which forfeits the amount points on wet-forecast days. See it play out on [Ray's Weather vs Open-Meteo, scored day by day](/right-wrong-ray).

The misses cluster on volatile mountain days, where a blown overnight low or an over-called snow total drags the score down. The spread between his best and worst days is wide.

## What is the most accurate forecast for Boone, NC?

For short-range Boone forecasts, Open-Meteo is the most accurate source we track, at 92.0 of 100 over 118 days with no Wrong days. Apple Weather is a close, no-setup second at 88.3. The provisional leaders (Met.no, Google, Visual Crossing) score higher still but on only nine days each, so we would not crown them yet.

### Which forecast wins short-range?

Open-Meteo wins the window and holds up over the long haul: 91.8 across 484 days. It is free, its API is open, and its numbers are reproducible from the public data. For a Boone resident who just wants the most accurate next-day read, it is the pick on this record.

### A fair word on lead time (short-range only)

One honest limit: everything here is short-range. We capture each forecast around midday and score it against the next day's actuals, so this measures next-day accuracy, not seven- or ten-day accuracy. Forecast skill decays with lead time for every source. That is settled meteorology, not something we measured. For that longer view, see [how accurate a 10-day forecast really is](/resources/articles/how-accurate-is-a-10-day-forecast).

## So should you still use Ray's Weather?

Use whatever you like. But if next-day accuracy is the goal, the free apps have the edge on this record. Ray's Weather still offers hyperlocal color, storm-by-storm narrative, and a mountain read that longtime users value, and none of that shows up in a single accuracy number. What the tracker shows is narrow and specific: on the four fields we score, a free app beats the paid one by about 21 points a day. Both things can be true at once.
