## The pitch

Ray's Weather will give you a nice little weather badge for your website, free of charge. Pick a station,
match your colors, copy the `<iframe>`, paste it on your site. Current conditions and a short forecast,
right there on your homepage. Hundreds of local businesses, cabins, and chamber pages run one.

It's a good deal. It's just not a free one — and the invoice is in the Terms of Use, which most people
who paste the code never read. We read it.

## What you actually agree to

From the Magnet Terms of Use, verbatim:

> "You must maintain the link back to the corresponding forecast page on RaysWeather.com. Do not use
> `rel="noreferrer"` or other attributes that would prevent referral information."

Translated: the widget is a link-building tool, and you're the one building the link. Every site that
embeds it hands Ray a backlink — the currency search engines use to decide who ranks. You're not the
customer here. You're the distribution.

And:

> "The Magnet collects limited analytics (impressions, clicks, IP address, and a pseudonymous visitor
> identifier)."

So every visitor to *your* page also pings *his* server, and he logs them. Not the end of the world —
lots of embeds do this — but worth knowing that the free badge on your cabin-rental site is quietly
reporting your visitors' IP addresses to a third party you didn't think of as a third party.

There's also a cap — three widgets per account — and a clause letting him switch yours off "at our sole
discretion." Which is his right. It's his widget. That's exactly the point: it's his widget, on your site,
doing his work.

## Why he does it

None of this is sinister. It's smart. A backlink from a real local business is worth money you can't
easily buy, and Ray's has quietly turned "here's a free weather badge for your website" into a network of
them. It's one of the oldest moves in the local-SEO playbook, and he runs it well.

We just think you should know the trade before you make it: a link and a little visitor data, in exchange
for a weather box you could get anywhere.
