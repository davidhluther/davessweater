## The claim on the tin

Ray's Weather sells itself on hyper-local coverage — "a 7-day forecast for 70+ locations" across the High
Country. Boone, Beech Mountain, Mt. Mitchell, Linville, Sparta, West Jefferson, and dozens more, each with
its own page, its own forecast, its own write-up.

That's the pitch. We wanted to see what "its own forecast" actually means, so on July 7 we pulled the
written forecast for all 66 locations at the same moment.

## Sixty-six locations. Three forecasts.

Not 66 forecasts. Not 50. **Three.**

Every one of the 66 locations was served one of exactly three written narratives — one for the mountains,
one for the foothills, one for the Asheville basin — each signed by the same author, each stamped across
dozens of "individual" town pages.

Here's the part that's hard to unsee. In the mountain group, **Mt. Mitchell — at 6,684 feet, the highest
peak east of the Mississippi — got the exact same written forecast as downtown Boone**, 3,400 feet lower.
Word for word. Beech Mountain, Sugar Mountain, and Linville Ridge got it too. A 4,000-foot spread of
elevation, one paragraph of weather.

We're not paraphrasing or characterizing. The text is byte-for-byte identical — we checked it
programmatically, then checked it again to be sure it wasn't a fluke.

## What's actually different between towns

The numbers. Ray's does attach a genuine per-town high and low — Mt. Mitchell ran 26 degrees colder than
Hickory the day we looked, which is real and correct. So the *data* underneath is per-location. It's the
*forecast* — the part a person writes, the part you actually read — that's one-size-fits-a-region.

Which is a defensible way to run a weather service! Writing 66 genuinely distinct daily narratives is a
lot of work for a seven-person shop. But it's worth knowing that the "local forecast" for your town is, in
the part that's written in English, the same forecast they gave a mountaintop an hour away.
